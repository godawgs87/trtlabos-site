Biomarker guide

The 14 markers.
Why every one matters.

Every biomarker TRT LabOS tests — what it measures, why it's specifically relevant to TRT, and which panel includes it. Not a generic wellness panel. Built around what actually changes on testosterone.

Core hormones

Included in both panels
Total Testosterone
Total T · ng/dL
Both panels

The primary male sex hormone and TRT's main target. Total T measures all testosterone in your blood — bound and unbound. It tells you whether your dose is delivering, and tracks how that delivery changes over time. Most clinics target 700–1,100 ng/dL on TRT, but the right number depends on your individual response, not just the reference range.

Watch for: Levels below target suggesting under-dosing or rapid clearance; levels above 1,200 ng/dL warranting a dose review.
Free Testosterone
Free T · pg/mL
Both panels

Only 2–3% of testosterone is "free" — unbound and biologically active. The rest is bound to SHBG and albumin and unavailable for cellular use. You can have a strong Total T and still feel terrible if your SHBG is high and Free T is low. This marker explains why two people on the same dose feel completely different.

Watch for: Low Free T despite adequate Total T — a sign of high SHBG pulling bioavailable hormone out of circulation.
Estradiol
E2 · pg/mL
Both panels

The primary estrogen in men, made when testosterone converts to estradiol via the aromatase enzyme. TRT raises E2 — some elevation is healthy and necessary. Too high causes water retention, mood instability, and low libido. Too low (often from over-managing with aromatase inhibitors) causes joint pain, brain fog, and equally low libido. Balance, not elimination, is the goal.

Watch for: E2 above 50 pg/mL with symptoms; E2 below 15 pg/mL — especially if on an aromatase inhibitor.
SHBG
Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin · nmol/L
Both panels

A protein that binds testosterone (and estradiol) and makes it unavailable for use. High SHBG means less free testosterone from the same dose — often requiring higher doses or more frequent injections. Low SHBG means faster clearance, often requiring smaller, more frequent doses to maintain stable levels. SHBG is one of the biggest drivers of why TRT protocols are individual.

Watch for: Trending up or down over time — SHBG shifts often explain why a stable protocol starts feeling different.

Pituitary signals

Intake panel only
These markers are suppressed to near-zero once exogenous testosterone is introduced. They're meaningful at baseline — not useful for recurring monitoring, which is why they're included in the intake panel only.
Luteinizing Hormone
LH · mIU/mL
Intake only

LH is the pituitary signal that tells your testes to produce testosterone. Before TRT, LH levels help assess whether low testosterone is primary (testicular failure) or secondary (pituitary signal problem) — a distinction that affects treatment options. Once TRT starts, exogenous testosterone suppresses LH to near-zero. Measuring it quarterly adds no clinical value on TRT.

Baseline significance: Low LH with low T suggests secondary hypogonadism (pituitary origin). Normal LH with low T suggests primary.
Follicle-Stimulating Hormone
FSH · mIU/mL
Intake only

FSH drives sperm production in the testes. Baseline FSH is important for understanding fertility status before starting TRT — exogenous testosterone significantly reduces FSH and sperm production. Like LH, FSH is suppressed by TRT and has no actionable value in routine monitoring of an established protocol.

Baseline significance: Pre-TRT FSH and LH together establish your hormonal starting point and fertility baseline for clinical records.

Blood safety

Both panels — critical ongoing TRT markers
Hematocrit
HCT · %
Both panels

Hematocrit is the percentage of your blood volume made up of red blood cells. Testosterone stimulates red blood cell production — beneficial in small amounts, dangerous when it goes too high. Elevated hematocrit thickens the blood, raising risk of clots, stroke, and cardiovascular events. Most TRT physicians want to see this below 52–54% and will pause or adjust therapy if it climbs above that threshold.

Watch for: Values above 52% — requires clinical attention. Above 54% generally warrants dose adjustment, frequency change, or therapeutic phlebotomy.
Hemoglobin
HGB · g/dL
Monitoring panelCBC at intake

Hemoglobin is the oxygen-carrying protein inside red blood cells. It rises in parallel with hematocrit on TRT, and tracking both together gives a more complete picture of blood thickening risk. It's measured individually in the monitoring panel and as part of the full CBC (Complete Blood Count) in the intake panel.

Watch for: Hemoglobin above 18.5 g/dL in men, especially in combination with elevated hematocrit.

Prostate health

Both panels
PSA
Prostate-Specific Antigen · ng/mL
Both panels

PSA is a protein produced by the prostate gland. It's used as a prostate health marker and an early indicator of prostate issues including cancer. The current clinical consensus is that TRT does not cause prostate cancer — but it can accelerate existing disease. A baseline PSA establishes your starting point. Ongoing monitoring watches for unexpected rises that warrant further evaluation. Essential for any man over 40 on TRT, and for establishing a record before starting therapy at any age.

Watch for: A rise of more than 0.75 ng/mL per year, or any single result above 4.0 ng/mL, should prompt urological consultation.

Cardiovascular risk

Both panels — as Lipid Panel (intake) and Lipid Risk Score (monitoring)
Total Cholesterol
TC · mg/dL
Both panels

The sum of all cholesterol in your blood. TRT can shift lipid balance, so total cholesterol provides the macro-level picture. It's most useful in context with LDL, HDL, and triglycerides — a high total cholesterol driven by high HDL is very different from high LDL.

Watch for: Total cholesterol above 240 mg/dL, interpreted alongside the rest of the lipid panel.
LDL Cholesterol
LDL · mg/dL
Both panels

Low-density lipoprotein — the primary driver of arterial plaque. TRT, particularly at higher doses, can raise LDL in some individuals. Tracking LDL over time catches a trend early, before it becomes a cardiovascular event. Important for anyone with existing cardiovascular risk factors.

Watch for: LDL above 130 mg/dL (borderline high) or an upward trend over consecutive tests.
HDL Cholesterol
HDL · mg/dL
Both panels

High-density lipoprotein — the "good" cholesterol that removes LDL from arteries. TRT, especially injectable testosterone, has a known tendency to lower HDL. A declining HDL trend is a meaningful cardiovascular signal and a reason some physicians recommend lifestyle modifications or protocol adjustments.

Watch for: HDL below 40 mg/dL, or a consistent downward trend over monitoring tests.
Triglycerides
TG · mg/dL
Both panels

Blood fats that reflect dietary intake and metabolic health. Elevated triglycerides often accompany insulin resistance, poor diet, and excess body fat — all common in hypogonadal men before TRT. TRT can improve body composition and sometimes lower triglycerides, but the relationship varies. Tracking over time shows whether your protocol is improving or worsening metabolic health.

Watch for: Triglycerides above 150 mg/dL (borderline), with particular concern above 200 mg/dL.

Metabolic baseline

Intake panel only
TSH and CMP are included at intake to establish a complete metabolic picture. TRT does not directly affect thyroid function or kidney/liver markers in most patients — making quarterly re-testing unnecessary and clinically redundant.
TSH
Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone · mIU/L
Intake only

TSH controls thyroid output, which affects metabolism, energy, body temperature, and mood. Undiagnosed hypothyroidism is common and makes TRT feel much less effective — you can optimize testosterone and still feel exhausted if your thyroid is underperforming. Screening at intake catches this before attributing symptoms to TRT alone. TRT itself doesn't meaningfully change TSH, so repeat testing isn't part of routine TRT monitoring.

Baseline significance: TSH above 4.0 mIU/L suggests hypothyroidism worth addressing before or alongside TRT optimization.
CMP — Liver & Kidney Function
Comprehensive Metabolic Panel
Intake only

The CMP covers kidney markers (creatinine, BUN, eGFR), liver enzymes (ALT, AST), electrolytes (sodium, potassium), and blood glucose. At intake, this establishes organ health before TRT and screens for conditions that could affect therapy. Injectable testosterone at physiologic doses doesn't significantly stress kidney or liver function in most patients — unlike oral 17-alpha alkylated androgens — so CMP is not part of the standard monitoring panel.

Baseline significance: Elevated liver enzymes or impaired kidney function at intake warrant further evaluation before starting TRT.
Panel comparison
What's in each panel — and why

The monitoring panel isn't a reduced version of the intake panel — it's focused on the markers that actively change with TRT and require tracking. Intake-only markers are either suppressed by TRT (LH, FSH) or don't respond meaningfully to it (TSH, CMP).
